BRAIN

RESEARCHERS AT UCLA USED FMRI TO SCAN THE BRAINS OF 32 TEENAGERS WHILE THEY BROWSED A SIMULATED INSTAGRAM-LIKE PLATFORM.
EACH PHOTO WAS SHOWN WITH EITHER A HIGH OR A LOW NUMBER OF LIKES.

PHOTOS WITH MORE LIKES WERE SIGNIFICANTLY MORE LIKELY TO RECEIVE ADDITIONAL LIKES FROM PARTICIPANTS, REGARDLESS OF CONTENT QUALITY.

VIEWING PHOTOS WITH MANY LIKES ACTIVATED THE NUCLEUS ACCUMBENS, THE BRAIN'S REWARD CENTRE.

THE SOCIAL INFLUENCE OF THE LIKE COUNT WAS STRONGER THAN THE PARTICIPANTS' OWN AESTHETIC JUDGEMENT OF THE CONTENT.

LIKE REMOVAL

IN 2019, INSTAGRAM RAN A LARGE-SCALE EXPERIMENT IN SEVERAL COUNTRIES IN WHICH LIKE COUNTS WERE HIDDEN FROM PUBLIC VIEW. ONLY THE PERSON WHO POSTED THE CONTENT COULD SEE THEIR OWN LIKE COUNT;
OTHERS COULD NOT.

MANY USERS REPORTED FEELING LESS ANXIOUS ABOUT POSTING WHEN THE PUBLIC COUNT WAS HIDDEN.

A SIGNIFICANT PORTION OF INFLUENCERS AND CONTENT CREATORS OPPOSED THE CHANGE, BECAUSE THEIR COMMERCIAL VALUE AND BRAND PARTNERSHIPS DEPENDED ON VISIBLE ENGAGEMENT METRICS.

[INSTAGRAM ULTIMATELY REVERSED THE CHANGE IN MOST MARKETS, CITING USER PREFERENCE]
